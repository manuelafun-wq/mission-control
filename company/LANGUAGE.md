# Language

How we communicate — in the product, in the team, and with the world.

Language is not secondary to design. It is design. The words we choose determine how people feel. They set the tone, build the trust, and define the experience as much as any color or layout.

At Mission Control, language is held to the same standard as everything else.

---

## The principles

### Warm.

Every piece of communication should feel like it comes from a person who genuinely cares. Not performed warmth — real warmth. The warmth of someone who is paying attention and wants things to go well for you.

This applies to Noah's voice, to error messages, to documentation, to how we write to each other internally. Everything carries the same temperature.

### Clear.

If something requires re-reading, it is not clear enough.

We say what we mean, directly, without burying the point. We do not write around things. We do not soften content into meaninglessness. Clear does not mean cold — it means the reader immediately understands.

### Short.

We respect people's time and attention.

We use the fewest words that do the job well. Not fewer — that would sacrifice clarity. But not more. Every extra word is a small tax on the reader. We do not charge that tax without reason.

### Respectful.

We do not talk down. We do not over-explain. We do not assume the reader needs to be managed.

The people who use Mission Control are intelligent, capable, ambitious. We write to that person. We trust them to understand, to decide, to act.

### Thoughtful.

Before we write something, we consider: how will this land? Not just what does it say — how does it make someone feel to read it?

Thoughtful language considers context. It considers the person reading at 7am before their first coffee, or at 10pm when they are tired and trying to close the day. It meets them where they are.

---

## What we never do

**Never create stress.**
Mission Control is designed to reduce cognitive load. Language that creates urgency, alarm, or anxiety undermines the entire product. If a message makes the reader tense, we rewrite it.

**Never overwhelm.**
We do not dump information. We surface what is needed, when it is needed, in the quantity required. More is not more. More is noise.

**Never use unnecessary complexity.**
Technical jargon, corporate language, filler phrases — none of these belong here. Not in the product, not in documentation, not in team communication.

Words we do not use: "going forward," "action items," "synergy," "circle back," "leverage," "touch base," "bandwidth" (as a metaphor for time), "deep dive" (as a verb).

Words we use: words that mean what they say.

## Noah's voice

Noah speaks Dutch to Manuela by default, unless she switches language.

His voice is: calm, direct, warm without being familiar, confident without being arrogant.

He speaks in short sentences. He favors the active voice. He says what needs to be said and stops.

He does not perform competence. He does not reassure unnecessarily. He does not use pleasantries as filler.

When Noah speaks, the reader should feel: *this person knows what they are doing, and they are looking out for me.*

## Internal communication

The same principles apply inside the team.

We write to each other the way we write to our users: warm, clear, short, respectful, thoughtful.

No one at Mission Control should ever feel anxious after reading an internal message. No one should feel overwhelmed, confused, or talked down to.

Mission Control should feel like the best workplace someone has ever experienced. Language is a large part of why.

---

## The test

Before sending any piece of communication — a label, a Noah message, a document, a message to a colleague — ask:

1. Is this warm?
2. Is this clear?
3. Is this as short as it can be while remaining both?
4. Does it respect the reader's intelligence?
5. Does it create calm, or does it create noise?

If the answer to any of these is no: rewrite.
